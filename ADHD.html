What is ADHD?

ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder)

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition that involves persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. It starts in childhood (before age 12 in most cases) and often continues into adulthood. Unlike normal restlessness or occasional distraction, ADHD symptoms are chronic, excessive, and impair daily functioning.

Difference Between Normal Behavior and ADHD

Criterion Normal Behavior ADHD
Trigger Distraction or high energy appears during boredom, fatigue, or excitement Difficulties persist regardless of environment or motivation
Duration Short-term, context based Symptoms last ≥6 months consistently
Severity & Functional Impact May affect performance sometimes Severe interference with school, work, and relationships
Physical & Cognitive Signs Occasional daydreaming, moving around Chronic forgetfulness, messy tasks, impulsivity, restlessness, low frustration tolerance
Response to Support Improvements with guidance or structure Limited improvement without structured interventions or medication
Nature of Thoughts Normal thought patterns Racing thoughts, difficulty slowing down, poor impulse control, emotional dysregulation

Symptoms of ADHD

Inattention Symptoms

  • Difficulty maintaining focus
  • Careless mistakes in school/work
  • Forgetting appointments, tasks, or belongings
  • Trouble organizing tasks and time
  • Easily distracted by external stimuli
  • Difficulty following through on instructions

Hyperactivity Symptoms

  • Fidgeting, inability to stay seated
  • Excessive running or climbing
  • Difficulty engaging in quiet activities
  • Always "on the go"
  • Talking excessively

Impulsivity Symptoms

  • Blurting out answers
  • Interrupting others
  • Difficulty waiting for turn
  • Risk-taking behaviors
  • Emotional swings and irritability

Causes & Risk Factors

Biological Factors

  • Genetic predisposition (strong heritability)
  • Brain structure/function differences in prefrontal cortex
  • Dopamine/norepinephrine imbalance
  • Premature birth or low birth weight
  • Prenatal exposure to nicotine, alcohol, or drugs

Psychological Factors

  • Chronic stress from failure in school/work
  • Coexisting conditions (anxiety, depression, learning disabilities)
  • Low self-esteem from repeated challenges

Social/Environmental Factors

  • Chaotic family environment
  • Lack of consistent discipline/structure
  • Exposure to environmental toxins
  • High-stress home or school environments

Diagnosis & Screening Tools

Diagnostic Process

  • Clinical interviews with patients, family, teachers
  • Behavioral observations in multiple settings
  • Medical evaluation to rule out other conditions
  • Assessment of developmental history

Screening Tools

  • Conners' Rating Scale
  • Vanderbilt ADHD Diagnostic Rating Scale
  • SNAP-IV Rating Scale
  • ADHD Rating Scale-5

DSM-5 Criteria

  • Symptoms present for ≥6 months
  • Onset before age 12
  • Symptoms present in ≥2 settings (home, school, work)
  • Clear evidence that symptoms interfere with functioning
  • Symptoms not better explained by another mental disorder

Treatment Options

Behavioral Therapy

Parent management training, social skills training, classroom strategies, organizational skills training

Medications

Stimulants: Methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta), Amphetamines (Adderall). Non-stimulants: Atomoxetine, Guanfacine

Lifestyle Changes

Structured daily routines, balanced diet, regular physical activity, good sleep hygiene

Other Treatments

Neurofeedback, CBT for ADHD, school/work accommodations, coaching

Self-Help & When to Seek Help

Self-Help & Coping Strategies

  • Use planners, alarms, and reminders
  • Break tasks into small, clear steps
  • Minimize distractions (organized desk, digital detox)
  • Regular physical exercise to reduce hyperactivity
  • Practice mindfulness/relaxation
  • Reward systems for task completion
  • Establish consistent routines
  • Use visual schedules and checklists

When to Seek Professional Help

  • If symptoms persist ≥6 months
  • If academic/work performance is declining
  • If relationships are strained due to impulsivity
  • If behaviors cause safety concerns (risk-taking, accidents)
  • If self-help strategies aren't sufficient
  • If ADHD symptoms are causing significant distress
  • If co-existing conditions are suspected (anxiety, depression)
